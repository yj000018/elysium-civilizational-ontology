## 6. Module Purpose

Deploy the patient/diagnosis metaphor to show that civilization requires integrated diagnosis, not symptom-by-symptom treatment. OPN-002 = systemic pattern; OPN-003 = diagnostic frame. Do not repeat OPN-002.

---

## 7. Opening Direction

> **Directional only — not a finished sentence.**

Open by introducing the patient metaphor — a doctor who treats symptoms separately vs. one who sees the whole patient — without repeating OPN-002's systemic argument

---

## 8. Closing Direction

> **Directional only — not a finished sentence.**

Close by establishing that civilization is the patient, and that the book is an attempt at integrated diagnosis — not a list of remedies but a map of the organism

---

## 9. Three Key Concepts to Deploy

1. **Whole-patient diagnosis:** The medical analogy: treating symptoms separately vs. diagnosing the whole patient — civilization requires the latter
2. **Integrated vs. fragmented medicine:** The structural critique of how we currently respond to civilizational problems — specialist by specialist, crisis by crisis
3. **The book as diagnosis:** Positioning ELYSIUM as a diagnostic map, not a manifesto or a list of solutions

---

## 10. Two Traps to Avoid

1. **Overlap with OPN-002:** OPN-002 = systemic pattern; OPN-003 = patient/diagnosis metaphor. Do not repeat the structural argument — deploy the metaphor
2. **Overextending the medical analogy:** Use the patient metaphor as a frame, not a theory — do not push it too far

---

## 11. Tone / Register

Metaphorical, accessible, slightly warmer than OPN-002. The medical metaphor should feel illuminating, not clinical.

---

## 12. Depth / Density

Medium. The metaphor does the work — do not over-explain.

---

## 13. Narrative Beats

1. Introduce the patient metaphor through a concrete medical scenario
2. Show the failure of symptom-by-symptom treatment
3. Apply to civilization: we have been treating symptoms, not the patient
4. The book's ambition: integrated diagnosis of the civilizational patient
5. Close: this requires a new kind of map — not a list of problems but a map of the organism
